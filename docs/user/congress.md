# Congress Tracker — User Guide

## What does it do?
Tracks stock trades made by US Congress members. Under the STOCK Act, all senators and representatives must publicly disclose personal stock trades within 45 days. This dashboard aggregates those disclosures so you can see what politicians are betting on.

## Tabs

### Trade Feed
A chronological list of all recent congressional trades, filterable by:
- **Chamber** — House, Senate, or both
- **Transaction type** — Purchases only, sales only, or all
- **Ticker** — Filter to a specific stock
- **Time period** — Last 30, 90, or 365 days

Each trade shows the member name, party, the stock traded, whether it was a purchase or sale, the reported dollar range, and the current price.

### Top Members
Ranks Congress members by trading activity. Shows:
- Total number of trades
- Purchase vs sale breakdown
- Total disclosed value
- Which stocks they've traded

### Hot Tickers
Which stocks are Congress members trading the most? Shows:
- Total trades per stock
- Buy/sell breakdown
- **Sentiment** — Bullish (more buys), Bearish (more sells), Mixed

### Summary
Overall statistics for the selected time period: total trades, unique members active, unique tickers traded, most active member and stock.

## How to use it

**Follow the purchases:** Like insider buying, congressional purchases are more meaningful than sales. A senator buying $500K of a stock in a regulated industry (healthcare, defense, tech) can be informative.

**Watch for clusters:** Multiple members from the same committee buying the same stock is a stronger signal than a single trade.

**Filter by committee relevance:**
- Defense committee members buying defense stocks
- Finance committee members buying bank stocks
- Energy committee members trading oil companies

These could reflect privileged knowledge about upcoming legislation or contracts.

## Important caveats
- **Disclosure delay:** Trades are disclosed up to 45 days after they happen. By the time you see it, the insider has already bought.
- **Dollar ranges are approximate:** Congress reports ranges (e.g., $15,001–$50,000), not exact amounts.
- **Not necessarily illegal:** Having information advantage is legal for Congress members in many situations (unlike corporate insiders).
- **Party ≠ direction:** Trading decisions are individual, not partisan.

## Legal context
The STOCK Act (2012) requires disclosure but does NOT prohibit trading on legislative knowledge. This remains a controversial legal gray area.
