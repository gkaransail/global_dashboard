# Earnings Calendar — User Guide

## What does it do?
Shows upcoming earnings dates and — critically — what the options market is pricing as the expected price move for each company. Also shows how the stock has historically reacted to earnings, so you can judge whether the current options pricing is reasonable.

## Tabs

### Calendar
A forward-looking view of upcoming earnings across major stocks in the next 30 days.

For each stock you see:
- **Earnings date** and whether it's Before Market Open (BMO) or After Market Close (AMC)
- **Expected Move %** — What the options market currently implies the stock will move by expiration (up or down)
- **Historical Avg Move** — What the stock has actually moved on average over the last 8 quarters
- **Expensive flag** — If current options are pricing in 20%+ more than historical average, they may be overpriced

### Earnings Analysis
Deep dive on a single stock:

**EPS History (last 8 quarters):**
- Analyst estimate vs actual EPS
- Surprise % (how much they beat/missed)
- Price reaction on the day of earnings

**Key Stats:**
- **Beat rate** — How often does this company beat EPS estimates? (>75% = historically reliable beater)
- **Avg move on beat** — When they beat, how much does the stock move?
- **Avg move on miss** — When they miss, how bad is the damage?
- **Current expected move** vs **historical average** — Is the market over- or under-pricing this earnings?

## How to use it

### Earnings plays
If expected move < historical average → options are relatively cheap (consider buying straddles/strangles for volatility)
If expected move > historical average × 1.2 → options are expensive (consider selling premium)

### Pre-earnings positioning
- High beat rate (>75%) + stock already up heading into earnings = "buy the rumor, sell the news" risk
- High beat rate + beaten-down stock = setup for a squeeze higher on a beat
- Company with history of big misses + high short interest = explosive move either way

### Position sizing
Use the expected move to size your position. If a $100 stock has a ±5% expected move, that's a ±$5 swing. Make sure your position size accounts for this volatility.

## Important caveats
- Expected move is for the nearest expiration, not specifically to the day after earnings
- EPS beats ≠ stock goes up. A beat below whisper numbers or weak guidance can send a stock down even on a technical beat
- Past earnings reactions don't guarantee future ones — sector conditions, macro environment, and stock positioning all matter
